Pakistan's summers are no joke. In Karachi, temperatures regularly exceed 40°C between May and August. Lahore and Islamabad are no different. For pets — especially dogs and cats that cannot sweat the way humans do — this level of heat can be genuinely dangerous. Heatstroke is one of the most common pet emergencies during summer months in Pakistan, and in many cases it is preventable.

This guide covers everything Pakistani pet owners need to know about keeping their animals safe and comfortable during the hottest months of the year.

## Why Pakistan's Summer is Especially Dangerous for Pets

Most pet care content online is written for Western audiences where summer means 28-30°C. In Pakistan, we are dealing with 40-45°C — a very different situation. Dogs and cats regulate their body temperature primarily through panting and limited sweating from their paw pads. When ambient temperatures approach body temperature (around 38-39°C for dogs), the body struggles to cool itself at all.

Breeds with flat faces — Pugs, French Bulldogs, Persian cats — are at even greater risk because their shortened airways make panting less efficient. If you own one of these breeds in Pakistan, summer requires extra vigilance.

## Keep Them Hydrated

Dehydration is the first step toward heatstroke. Your pet needs significantly more water in summer than in winter.

**Practical tips for Pakistani pet owners:**
- Provide at least two water bowls and place them in different rooms
- Refresh water every few hours — warm water is less appealing to drink
- Add ice cubes to the water bowl; most dogs find this exciting
- Consider a pet water fountain, which keeps water cool and circulating
- If your pet is outdoors, check the water bowl every hour in peak heat

Signs of dehydration include dry or sticky gums, sunken eyes, loss of skin elasticity (pinch the skin and it should snap back immediately), and lethargy. If you see these signs, offer water immediately and contact a vet.

## Watch the Paws — Pavements in Pakistan Are Extremely Hot

Concrete and asphalt in Pakistani cities absorb heat throughout the day. By midday, pavement in Karachi can reach 60-70°C — hot enough to cause serious burns in seconds.

**The simple test:** Place the back of your hand on the pavement for seven seconds. If you cannot keep it there, it is too hot for your dog's paws.

Walk your dog only during the early morning (before 8 AM) or after sunset. Avoid midday and afternoon walks entirely during peak summer. If your dog needs to go outside during daytime, consider dog booties — available from several pet stores in Karachi and Lahore, and orderable online.

## Never Leave Your Pet in a Parked Car

This cannot be stressed enough. Even with windows cracked, a car parked in the sun in Pakistan can reach 70-80°C within minutes. This kills pets. There is no safe amount of time to leave a pet in a parked car during Pakistani summer.

If you are running errands, leave your pet at home. If you are travelling, only take your pet if someone can stay with them in an air-conditioned vehicle at all times.

## Create Cool Spaces at Home

Most Pakistani homes use ceiling fans and split ACs. For your pet's benefit:

- Designate one air-conditioned room where your pet can always retreat
- If AC is not available, keep ceiling fans running and provide wet towels on the floor
- Cooling mats (gel-filled pads) are widely available at pet stores and reduce body temperature effectively
- A small shallow tub of cool water that your dog can stand in is extremely effective for large breeds
- Marble or tiled floors are naturally cooler — allow your pet to lie on them

Cats instinctively find the coolest spot in the house. Make sure they have unobstructed access to tiled floors, under beds, and other cool areas.

## Adjust Exercise Routines Completely

During summer in Pakistan, the concept of a midday walk should be abandoned entirely. Adjust your routine:

- **Morning walks**: Before 8 AM, when pavements are still cool from the night
- **Evening walks**: After 7 PM or once the sun has set
- **Indoor play**: Replace outdoor exercise with indoor games during peak heat hours
- **Reduce duration**: Even morning and evening walks should be shorter than usual

Dogs that are used to long runs should have their exercise significantly reduced during heatwave periods. Overexertion in heat is a common cause of heatstroke.

## Recognise the Signs of Heatstroke

Heatstroke is a medical emergency. Know the signs:

**Early warning signs:**
- Excessive, loud panting
- Drooling more than usual
- Restlessness, seeking cool surfaces compulsively
- Bright red gums and tongue

**Advanced signs (requires immediate vet visit):**
- Vomiting or diarrhoea
- Glazed eyes, confusion, or disorientation
- Muscle tremors or seizures
- Collapse or unconsciousness

**What to do if you suspect heatstroke:**
1. Move your pet to a cool, shaded area or air-conditioned room immediately
2. Offer small amounts of cool (not ice cold) water
3. Apply cool (not cold) wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin
4. Do NOT submerge in ice water — the shock can be dangerous
5. Get to a vet immediately

## Grooming and Coat Care in Summer

Many Pakistani pet owners shave their dogs in summer thinking it will keep them cooler. This is usually a mistake. A dog's double coat actually insulates them from heat as well as cold. Shaving removes this natural protection and can cause sunburn.

Instead:
- Brush regularly to remove dead undercoat which traps heat
- Keep long-haired cats and dogs professionally groomed
- Consult your vet before making any major grooming changes

Short-haired, light-coloured dogs can sunburn. If your dog spends time in direct sun, pet-safe sunscreen is available at specialist pet stores.

## Nutrition Changes in Summer

Pets often eat less in extreme heat — this is normal. Do not force-feed. However:

- Ensure they are still getting enough water even if eating less
- Wet food (canned) has higher moisture content and can help with hydration
- Avoid feeding your pet during the hottest part of the day
- Store dry pet food in cool, dry places to prevent spoilage

## A Note on Outdoor Pets

Stray cats and community dogs around your neighbourhood suffer severely in Pakistani summers. If you have the means, keeping a water bowl outside your home or building can genuinely save lives. Small acts of kindness make a real difference during heatwaves.

Summer in Pakistan is intense, but with the right precautions, your pet can get through it safely. When in doubt, cool them down and call a vet.

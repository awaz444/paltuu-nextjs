Dog training in Pakistan is growing rapidly as more families adopt dogs as companion animals rather than just guard dogs. But many Pakistani dog owners still feel uncertain about where to start with training, or believe that serious training requires a professional. It does not.

The five commands in this guide are the foundation of every well-behaved dog — and you can teach all of them at home, with treats from your kitchen, in 5-10 minute sessions over a few weeks.

## Why Training Matters in Pakistan's Context

Pakistan presents unique challenges for dog owners. Busy streets, large family households, frequent guests, loud environments — a dog that cannot follow basic commands is genuinely difficult to manage in Pakistani home life. A dog that knows "sit", "stay", and "leave it" is safer, calmer around guests, and far easier to manage in city traffic.

Training also strengthens the relationship between you and your dog. Dogs are pack animals — they want structure and clear communication. A dog that knows what is expected of it is a happier, less anxious dog.

## Before You Begin: The Golden Rules

**Use positive reinforcement only.** Punishing dogs — shouting, hitting, yanking the leash — is both ineffective and harmful. It creates fear and anxiety without teaching the dog what you *want* it to do. Every serious animal behaviourist and vet in Pakistan agrees: treats, praise, and positive reinforcement produce the best results, fastest.

**Keep sessions short.** 5-10 minutes maximum, 2-3 times per day. Dogs — especially puppies — lose focus quickly. End every session on a success, even if it means asking for a command you know your dog already knows well.

**Be consistent.** Every person in your household must use the same word and the same hand signal for each command. Using "baithna", "sit", and "baith ja" interchangeably for the same command confuses your dog. Pick one word and stick to it across the household.

**Choose the right treats.** High-value treats work best for training. In Pakistan, small pieces of boiled chicken work brilliantly. Commercial training treats (ProChoice, Pedigree treats) are widely available and convenient. The treat should be small — pea-sized — so your dog can eat it in one second and stay focused.

## Command 1: Sit

**Why it matters:** Sit is the foundation of every other command and the most immediately useful command in daily life. A dog that sits on command is easier to control when guests arrive, when you are putting on the leash, and when crossing the street.

### How to Teach Sit

1. Hold a treat close to your dog's nose
2. Slowly move your hand upward — the dog's nose follows the treat, and as the nose goes up, the bottom naturally goes down
3. The moment their bottom touches the floor, say "Sit" clearly once
4. Immediately give the treat and praise ("good dog", enthusiastic tone)
5. Repeat 5-10 times per session

**Common mistake:** Saying "Sit, sit, sit" repeatedly. Say it once. Repeating the command teaches the dog that they only need to respond eventually. One command, one response.

After 3-4 successful sessions, start adding the hand signal: an open palm facing upward, raised as you say "Sit". This lets you command your dog silently in noisy environments.

## Command 2: Stay

**Why it matters:** Stay keeps your dog in place when you need them to — at the door when guests arrive, when you need to answer a call, or when you need to prevent them from running into the street.

### How to Teach Stay

Sit must be solid before introducing Stay.

1. Ask your dog to Sit
2. Open your palm toward the dog's face (like a "stop" sign) and say "Stay"
3. Take one step back
4. Pause for 3 seconds
5. Return to the dog, reward and praise
6. Release with a word — "okay" or "free" — so the dog knows Stay is over

### Progressive Training Schedule

- **Week 1**: 3 seconds, 1 step away
- **Week 2**: 10 seconds, 3 steps away
- **Week 3**: 20-30 seconds, walking to another room
- **Week 4+**: Build toward 1 minute, across the room

**Key point:** Always return to the dog to reward — do not call them to you while teaching Stay. Calling them rewards breaking the Stay. Go back to them, pause, then release.

If your dog breaks Stay, do not punish. Simply reset — ask them to sit again and start from a shorter distance and time.

## Command 3: Come (Recall)

**Why it matters:** Come can save your dog's life. A dog that reliably comes when called can be called away from danger — traffic, another aggressive dog, something toxic on the ground. In Pakistan's busy streets, reliable recall is critical.

### How to Teach Come

1. Attach a long leash (5-6 metres) to your dog's collar
2. Allow the dog to wander a short distance
3. Crouch down to their level — this is less threatening and more inviting
4. Say "Come" in an excited, happy voice — never stern or angry
5. Gently reel in the leash as you call them
6. When they reach you, reward with the highest-value treat you have and enthusiastic praise
7. Never punish a dog when they come to you, even if they took a long time. Coming to you must always be a positive experience

**Critical rule:** Never call "Come" and then do something the dog dislikes — giving a bath, ending play, putting in a crate. If you do this, the dog associates Come with bad things and stops responding. Instead, call them, reward them, and then do what needs to be done.

### Building Strong Recall

Practice recall multiple times daily in the house and garden before relying on it outside. The more repetitions with consistent rewards, the faster and more reliable the recall becomes. A dog with excellent recall in Pakistan — where gates are often left open and streets are busy — is significantly safer than one without it.

## Command 4: Down

**Why it matters:** Down is a calming command. It is harder for a dog to maintain excitement when lying down. Useful for keeping your dog calm during meals, when guests sit, and for extended Wait situations.

### How to Teach Down

1. Ask for Sit first
2. Hold a treat in your closed fist at the dog's nose
3. Move your hand straight down to the floor
4. As the dog follows the treat, slide your hand along the floor away from them — the dog should lower into a down position to follow
5. The moment their elbows hit the floor, say "Down" and give the treat
6. If the dog stands up instead of lying down, go back a step — hold the treat closer to the chest, not moving away as quickly

Down is harder than Sit for many dogs because it is a more submissive position. Be patient. Some dogs take longer with this command. Never physically push the dog into a down position — it breaks trust.

## Command 5: Leave It

**Why it matters:** Leave It is potentially life-saving in Pakistan, where street food, bones, toxic plants, and other hazards are everywhere on the ground. A dog with solid Leave It can be told to ignore something dangerous before they pick it up.

### How to Teach Leave It

1. Place a treat in both hands
2. Show the dog one closed fist with a treat inside — let them sniff and paw at it
3. Say "Leave it" once
4. Ignore all attempts to get the treat from the closed fist — wait
5. The moment the dog looks away or loses interest in the closed fist, say "Yes!" and immediately give the treat from the *other* hand
6. The dog learns: ignoring the thing I want earns a reward from elsewhere

### Advancing Leave It

Once the dog understands the concept:
- Place a treat on the floor, cover with your hand. Say "Leave it" — remove your hand only when the dog looks at you instead of the treat
- Progress to treats on the floor without covering, then to treats and objects on walks

Leave It is one of the most practically important commands for Pakistani dog owners given the variety of hazardous materials (bones, spiced food, chemicals) accessible in urban environments.

## Training Mistakes to Avoid

**Inconsistent commands**: Different family members using different words is the single most common training failure in Pakistani households. Hold a family meeting and agree on the words before starting.

**Too long sessions**: 10 minutes maximum. Puppies under 6 months should do 5 minutes maximum.

**Training when the dog is tired, hungry (not a little hungry), or overstimulated**: The optimum state is slightly hungry (training before meals works well), calm, and in a relatively quiet environment initially.

**Expecting too much too fast**: A reliable Sit typically takes 1-2 weeks. A reliable Stay can take 4-6 weeks. Recall on the street takes months of consistent practice. Progress takes time.

**Stopping training**: Dogs forget commands that are not maintained. Five minutes of practice a few times a week is enough to keep all five commands sharp — forever.

## When to Seek Professional Help in Pakistan

Consider professional training if:
- Your dog shows any aggression (growling, snapping, biting)
- Basic commands are not working after 8+ weeks of consistent training
- Your dog has severe anxiety that prevents learning
- You have a working breed (German Shepherd, Belgian Malinois) with high drive that you are struggling to channel
